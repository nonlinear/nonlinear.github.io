I love the minimal artstyle, and how it portrays an underground, feel-good gay romance. It’s an easy read. {{< /recap>}} {{< recap id="02" title="Blue Period" image="blue-period-anime-name.avif" link="https://anilist.co/manga/107237/Blue-Period/" >}} It follows the journey of a character who transitions from being a sort of bad-boy jock into the world of art. He’s someone who’s always lived for others, striving to appear effortless and cool, and now he’s exploring his own identity through art. The manga dives into both the personal journey of self-discovery and the technical challenges of art, like composition, color, and the pressure of maintaining a vision. Eventually I let it go because it falls into the "life is hard do your best" of most edutainment manga. {{< /recap>}} {{< recap id="03" title="The Wandering of Girls' Pair" image="0xq3hoqfzl881.png" link="https://anilist.co/manga/53306/Dainana-Joshikai-Houkou/" >}} A zany manga set in a peculiar future where reality, dreams, and technology blend in playful, sometimes absurd ways. We have a ghost friend mediated by tech and warning robots from the future. Society will change as our interiority becomes legible to machines, so it's kind of cool to see a future that explore that weirdness with a sense of awe. {{< /recap>}} {{< recap id="04" title="Dorohedoro" image="dorohedoro-hayashida.jpg.webp" >}} Written and illustrated by Q Hayashida, this manga was serialized from 2000 to 2018. The series is known for its dark humor, complex world-building, and unique art style. Read in 2025. The manga is notable for blending gritty action with surreal elements. {{< /recap>}} {{< recap id="05" title="Given" image="given-2-e1590433754586.jpg" link="https://anilist.co/anime/108430/Given">}} Created by Natsuki Kizu, this manga started in 2013.
